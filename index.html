Strawberry & Soft Fruit

Strawberries and other soft fruit are among the most perishable items in the produce supply chain. They are vulnerable to compression damage, ethylene from neighboring produce, and rapid temperature fluctuation. Shallow PP corrugated produce boxes — typically 80–100 mm depth — provide a rigid, non-compressible structure that protects fragile berries better than a collapsing cardboard punnet holder.

Wax cardboard boxes have been standard for strawberry export but carry real structural risk in humid cold-store environments. A plastic produce box offers the same or better cold-chain performance without the collapse risk, and critically, can be cleaned between loads if cross-crop contamination is a concern.

For long-distance strawberry transport, look for boxes with maximum-coverage base panels (to support the weight of the punnet stack evenly) and side vent patterns that allow pre-cooling airflow without creating pressure points on the fruit.

Mango & Tropical Fruit

Tropical fruits including mangoes, papayas, and pineapple are typically transported from origin countries in regulated conditions that require robust, moisture-stable packaging. The high humidity environments common in tropical growing regions, combined with the cold shock of refrigerated container loading, create exactly the conditions where cardboard and wax boxes deteriorate fastest.

Plastic produce boxes — sized to tropical export standards, which often run slightly deeper than temperate-produce equivalents — maintain their structural integrity regardless of how wet the loading environment is. For mango specifically, where export protocols often require hydrocooling or icing, a plastic box that doesn't absorb water or collapse under ice loading is a significant operational advantage.

From a regulatory perspective, plastic boxes also present a cleaner ISPM 15 phytosanitary picture than wood-based crating, which must be heat-treated or fumigated for international transit. PP corrugated is exempt from ISPM 15 wood packaging requirements.

Citrus & Orange Packing

Oranges and other citrus fruit are high-volume, high-weight produce items that put real demands on box structure. A standard 600×400 mm Euro box packed with oranges may carry 18–22 kg of fruit weight. Over a 4–6 pallet height, the cumulative dynamic stacking load demands careful specification of box gauge and corner construction.

Wax cardboard boxes are widely used for citrus export but typically rated for single-use only. The economics of single-use boxes at citrus volumes — where a major grower might use hundreds of thousands of boxes per season — make a compelling case for the reusable plastic alternative. The initial investment in a plastic box pool is recovered quickly when the per-box cost is amortized over 50+ trips.

Citrus packing lines also benefit from the dimensional consistency of plastic produce boxes, which do not vary between batches the way cardboard can (particularly in humid climates where cardboard absorbs moisture and dimensions drift). Consistent dimensions improve packing line throughput and reduce jams in automated packing equipment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is wax produce box and why should I replace it?

A wax produce box is a corrugated cardboard box coated with wax (typically paraffin or soy wax) to improve moisture resistance in refrigerated transport. While this extends cardboard's useful life versus uncoated board, wax boxes are still single-use and cannot be recycled — the wax coating contaminates cardboard recycling streams. They go to landfill. Plastic PP corrugated produce boxes are fully reusable, outperform wax boxes in moisture resistance, and are recyclable at end of life.

How much does a plastic produce box cost compared to wax cardboard?

Plastic PP corrugated produce boxes typically cost 5–15× more per unit than a comparable wax cardboard box. However, plastic boxes are reused 50–200 times, bringing the per-trip packaging cost far below single-use wax cardboard. For operations running closed-loop logistics, the per-trip cost of plastic is usually 80–95% lower than wax cardboard on a cost-per-trip basis.

Can plastic produce boxes handle iced or hydrocooled produce?

Yes. Plastic PP corrugated is completely unaffected by water, ice, or hydrocooling processes. The material has near-zero moisture absorption (less than 0.1% by weight per ASTM D570). Boxes used in iced or hydrocooled applications maintain full structural integrity and can be immediately drained and returned to service after cooling treatment.

Are plastic produce boxes approved for food contact?

Food-grade PP resins are FDA-compliant for food contact and meet EU Regulation 10/2011 requirements for plastic materials in contact with food. Reputable suppliers will provide material compliance documentation. Unlike wood or fiber packaging, PP has no natural contaminants and its food-contact properties don't change with repeated washing.

What happens to plastic produce boxes at end of life?

Polypropylene (PP, resin code #5) is a widely recycled thermoplastic. At end of useful life, plastic produce boxes are ground into PP regrind, which is used to manufacture new plastic products. This contrasts favorably with wax-coated cardboard, which is a mixed material (wax + fiber) and typically cannot be separated for recycling — sending it to landfill or incineration.
